### Candidate 1: Iberian Peninsula Blackout — 1-Year Anniversary & ENTSO-E Final Report (March–April 2026)

**Source:** ENTSO-E Final Report (published 20 March 2026); Brussels Signal anniversary coverage (29 April 2026)
**URLs:**
- https://www.entsoe.eu/publications/blackout/28-april-2025-iberian-blackout/
- https://brusselssignal.eu/2026/04/spain-marks-one-year-since-europes-worst-blackout-in-decades-as-accountability-remains-elusive/
**Date:** Final report 20 March 2026; anniversary reporting 29 April 2026

**Summary:** 
The final ENTSO-E expert panel report on the 28 April 2025 Iberian blackout identified the causes as a combination of many interacting factors — including oscillations, gaps in voltage and reactive power control, differences in voltage regulation practices, rapid output reductions and generator disconnections in Spain, and uneven stabilisation capabilities — which led to fast increases of voltage, cascading generation disconnections, and the blackout across continental Spain and Portugal.
 
ENTSO-E underlined that the blackout exposed a fundamental tension in European energy policy: the drive for renewable energy integration has not always been matched by investment in the grid infrastructure and regulatory frameworks needed to keep systems stable.


One year on, 
none of the sanction proceedings opened against REE and ten other energy companies has resulted in a final ruling assigning responsibility as of April 28, 2026.


**SE/Systems Thinking Angle (non-obvious):**
This is a textbook **verification gap** case — but the non-obvious angle is deeper. The grid was technically performing to specification at every *local* level: solar was generating as contracted, automatic protection relays were tripping exactly as designed. The system-level failure was not in any individual component, but in the *emergent behaviour* of a system whose architecture had been quietly redesigned by the energy transition without a corresponding update to the system-level operational concept. The real ENTSO-E finding — 
"the problem is not renewable energy, but voltage control, regardless of the type of generation"
 — is a perfect SE framing: the *interface* between generation nodes and grid stability had been allowed to drift out of specification as generation architecture changed faster than the control architecture. It is also a multi-stakeholder requirements conflict: political requirements ("100% renewables") were set without adequate systems engineering to define the new operational envelope. 
Missing fault-recording data from key generation owners made root-cause determination impossible, and many events occurred within a very short timeframe, making it difficult to determine their exact sequence, as not all data was time-synchronised with sufficient accuracy.
 An SE audience will immediately recognise the "no observable state = no diagnosability" problem.

**Date:** 11 March 2026 (IEA decision), ongoing into April 2026

**Summary:** 
The 32 Member countries of the IEA unanimously agreed to make 400 million barrels of oil from their emergency reserves available to the market to address disruptions stemming from the war in the Middle East — a decision made following an extraordinary meeting convened by the IEA Executive Director to assess market conditions and consider options to address supply disruptions.
 
Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz disrupted 20% of global oil supplies and significant LNG volumes; even after Iran and the United States announced a ceasefire on 8 April, ship traffic through the Strait remained far below pre-war levels.


**SE/Systems Thinking Angle (non-obvious):**
The non-obvious angle here is about **global energy supply as a system-of-systems with a single-point-of-failure architecture that was visible but un-mitigated**. 
About 20 million barrels of crude oil and petroleum products transit the Strait of Hormuz each day, equivalent to roughly 20% of global oil consumption; even with the massive emergency release, analysts said that strategic reserves can cover only a fraction of the supply loss if the conflict dragged on, with one analyst noting that "IEA drawdowns can at best only offset a fraction of the roughly 15 million barrels per day net supply loss."
 The SE angle: the IEA emergency stockpile mechanism is a **balancing feedback loop** — but the reserves were sized and designed for the threat models of the 1970s. 
Each IEA country has an obligation to hold oil stocks equivalent to at least 90 days of net oil imports.
 The system was designed for a partial disruption of a few weeks; a near-total Hormuz closure exceeds the design envelope. This is a **requirements volatility** case: the operational context changed (full chokepoint closure by a state actor during active combat), but the reserve architecture was never re-scoped to match.
