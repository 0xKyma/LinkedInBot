### Option 1 — Practitioner Angle

An AI agent that follows RFLP methodology, asks you clarifying questions, and builds your System Composer model from a conversation.

That's not a concept paper. That's a working demo MathWorks published four days ago.

The setup: a coding agent connects to MATLAB and Simulink via MCP servers, ingests requirements from Excel or elicits them conversationally, then structures the model according to RFLP. You describe what you want. It asks what it needs. The model takes shape.

I've been skeptical of "AI + MBSE" claims for two years, and I'm still skeptical of most of them. But there's something worth taking seriously here — not the automation itself, but what it exposes.

If an agent can follow RFLP methodology from a prompt, it means the methodology is explicit enough to encode. That's actually a forcing function for the engineering team. Vague, undisciplined SE processes won't survive contact with an agent that needs unambiguous instructions.

The real question isn't whether the AI can do this. It's whether your team's methodology is rigorous enough to hand to one.

The author is upfront that skepticism is warranted. But I think the skepticism needs to be more specific than "AI bad." What part of your current MBSE workflow would break if an agent tried to follow it — and what does that tell you?

### Option 2 — Industry/Trend Angle

Agentic AI just started showing up in MBSE toolchains. The SE community is not ready for what that question forces us to answer.

MathWorks published a working demonstration last week — an agent that drives an MBSE workflow using RFLP, conversational requirements elicitation, and live model construction in System Composer. It's real, it runs, and it points at something larger than one vendor demo.

Here's the trajectory I'm watching: for a decade, the gap between SE methodology and modeling practice has been papered over by experienced engineers who held the rigor in their heads. The model was a documentation artifact. The real system knowledge lived in the people.

Agentic AI breaks that arrangement wide open — not because the agent is smarter than the engineers, but because it can only work with what's explicit. It cannot inherit tribal knowledge. It cannot fill gaps from intuition. It will faithfully execute whatever process you give it, including a broken one.

That is going to accelerate the divide between programs with genuinely rigorous MBSE implementations and programs that have been performing MBSE theater.

The industry conversation right now is about which tools support AI agents. The more uncomfortable conversation is about which SE organizations actually have the process discipline those agents would require.

Which one does your program have?

### Option 3 — Contrarian Angle

If an AI agent can drive your MBSE workflow, your workflow might not be doing what you think it is.

I don't mean that as a criticism of the MathWorks demo published last week — it's technically interesting and the team is transparent about its limitations. I mean it as a structural observation about methodology.

RFLP is a defined, sequential decomposition pattern. It's encodable because it's explicit. And that's exactly the kind of workflow where AI agents will perform well and look impressive in demos.

What agents cannot do — at least not yet — is the hard part of systems engineering: recognizing when the requirements structure is wrong, catching emergent interface issues that don't show up until you're three levels deep, or knowing when the customer's stated need and their actual operational problem are different things.

Those aren't workflow steps. They're judgment calls. They're why senior systems engineers cost what they do.

My concern isn't that agentic AI will replace SE expertise. My concern is that it makes methodological compliance look like methodological competence. A model that is structurally complete and agent-generated is not automatically a model that reflects good systems thinking.

The MBSE community has already struggled to distinguish model quantity from model quality. Agents will make that problem worse before they make it better.

What's the one judgment call in your current program that you would not trust an agent to make — and are you certain a junior engineer isn't already making it on autopilot?
