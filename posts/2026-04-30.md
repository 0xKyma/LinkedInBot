An AI agent that does MBSE. The demo works. The methodology compliance claim deserves scrutiny anyway.

The humans reviewing agent output need more methodology expertise than the ones reviewing human output — not less.

## C4 — arXiv: "Uncertainty Modeling for SysML v2" (PSUM-SysMLv2)

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle
Every MBSE model I've reviewed encodes uncertainty the same way: it doesn't.

Requirements land with exact values. Interface margins get a safety factor. The model looks complete. What it actually contains is a precise representation of assumptions nobody has validated.

A February preprint proposes PSUM-SysMLv2 — a structured extension that brings OMG's new Precise Semantics for Uncertainty Modeling directly into SysML v2 constructs. Seven case studies. Formal validation. No conformance breakage.

The OMG published PSUM specifically because the SE community needed a standardized way to represent uncertainty that isn't just a comment or a tagged value someone reads and ignores. Most practitioners I work with don't know PSUM exists yet.

If your program uses SysML v2 and operates in a safety-critical domain, this is the paper to read before your next architecture review.

What's your current practice for surfacing parameter uncertainty inside a SysML model — and is it actually traceable?

Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2602.21641

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle
SysML v2 improved semantic rigor significantly. It still has no native constructs for uncertainty. That gap now has a proposed fix — and an OMG standard behind it.

The OMG published PSUM — Precise Semantics for Uncertainty Modeling — as a formal specification for representing uncertainty within systems models. It is the first standardized treatment of this problem in the MBSE community. A recent preprint demonstrates how to integrate PSUM directly into SysML v2 without breaking conformance, validated across seven engineering case studies.

This is the trajectory: SysML v2 as a foundation that specialized semantic extensions are built on top of, each OMG-standardized, each composable. Uncertainty today. Probabilistic behavior next.

The question for tooling vendors is whether SysML v2 implementation roadmaps are tracking OMG's extension ecosystem — or just chasing the base spec.

Which domain do you think needs first-class uncertainty representation in SysML v2 most urgently: safety analysis, early design trade studies, or interface margin tracking?

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle
We built SysML v2 with significantly improved precision. We then used it to model uncertain systems as if they weren't uncertain. That is a methodology problem, not a language problem.

PSUM-SysMLv2 is technically sound and I think the community should follow it. But the reason we need a formal uncertainty extension is that the practice of MBSE has systematically treated uncertainty as a pre-modeling concern — something resolved before the architecture is committed to the model, rather than something the model should represent and propagate.

Safety analysis teams carry the uncertainty. Simulation teams carry it. The architecture model holds the point estimates and calls it done.

Adding PSUM constructs to SysML v2 is the right technical move. But without a change in how programs value and review uncertainty representation, the extension will be adopted by researchers and ignored in production models — the same way most SysML constraints are today.

When did your program last update its architecture model to reflect a change in a key parameter's uncertainty bounds — not just its nominal value?

## Track 2: World Events — Defence / Energy / Geopolitics (SE Lens)

### World Event Option 1 — Verification Gap + Interface Management Frame

Zeroing a programme's budget mid-qualification isn't a funding decision. It's an uncontrolled test exit.

The Army's T901 engine needs roughly 5,000 hours of qualification testing to be certified inside a rotorcraft. The FY27 budget request allocates zero dollars to complete it. The engine has flown. It works. But an engine that hasn't completed qualification against its host platform isn't a fielded capability — it's a very expensive prototype.

What makes this worse: Boeing is spending its own IRAD money to integrate the T901 onto the Apache drivetrain, because the Apache can't absorb the T901's increased horsepower without drivetrain modifications. That's not a late discovery. That's an interface that was never formally closed.

We've spent an estimated $1.5 billion and two decades on this programme. The verification gap isn't the risk. The gap *is* the programme's current state.

When a budget decision terminates a test programme mid-cycle, who owns the requirement to document what was *not* verified — and what that means for the platform that was supposed to host it?

### World Event Option 2 — Feedback Loops + Unintended Consequences Frame

A programme can be technically sound and still get killed by a system it was never designed to survive.

The T901 engine flew. It outperforms the engine it was meant to replace. But the Army is now questioning whether the Apache's future role — integrated with autonomous launched effects — looks anything like the mission context that justified the engine requirement in the first place. The operational context shifted faster than the qualification lifecycle could follow.

Meanwhile, the budget loop runs on its own rhythm entirely. Pentagon zeros it. Congress restores it. Pentagon zeros it again. That oscillation isn't dysfunction — it's the emergent behaviour of two systems (acquisition strategy and annual appropriations) that were never designed to be coupled, but are.

The T901 isn't caught in a funding gap. It's caught between at least three systems — requirements, verification, and budget — each operating on a different cycle with no shared feedback mechanism.

The uncomfortable question for anyone running a long-cycle programme: which of your external systems has the power to invalidate your SE lifecycle — and have you modelled it?
