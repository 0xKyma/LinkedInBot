## C1 — MathWorks: MBSE + Agentic AI (RFLP workflow with Claude Code)

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

An AI agent just ran a full RFLP workflow in System Composer. Here's what that actually means for MBSE practice.

Sarah Dagen at MathWorks built an agentic workflow using Claude Code and the Simulink MCP Server to automate the Requirements–Functional–Logical–Physical methodology end-to-end. It's not a demo. The skills are downloadable and executable.

The part that caught my attention: the author notes that most humans aren't enthusiastic about complying with modelling standards. So maybe letting an agent handle that part is reasonable.

That's a real observation. Methodology compliance is where MBSE programmes quietly fall apart. If an agent can enforce RFLP consistently, that's worth taking seriously, regardless of how you feel about AI in the loop.

What I'm still working through is where V&V traceability lands when an agent generates the structural decomposition. That's not a reason to dismiss this. It's the right question to ask before you adopt it.

Programmes that have invested in clear, documented methodology are positioned to benefit from this immediately. Programmes that haven't will find that an agent enforces their ambiguity just as consistently as their rigour.

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

We've spent years arguing about whether engineers will actually follow MBSE methodology. The answer, apparently, is to stop asking them to.

The MathWorks post on agentic AI and RFLP is technically impressive. Claude Code, the Simulink MCP Server, end-to-end RFLP automation. I'm not dismissing it.

But I want to name something the enthusiasm is glossing over. Methodology compliance was never just a process problem. It was a signal. When engineers skip steps or deviate from RFLP, that deviation often carries information: the methodology doesn't fit the problem, the model structure is wrong, or the system understanding isn't there yet.

An agent that enforces compliance consistently will also suppress that signal consistently.

That doesn't make this work less valuable. It makes the human review layer more important, not less. The question isn't whether agents can run RFLP. It's whether your team will know when the agent got it wrong.

## C2 — arXiv: Uncertainty Modeling for SysML v2 (PSUM-SysMLv2)

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

SysML v2 is expressive. But uncertainty isn't a first-class citizen in it, and for autonomous or cyber-physical systems, that gap is real.

A recent arXiv preprint proposes PSUM-SysMLv2: a structured extension that integrates the OMG's Precise Semantics for Uncertainty Modelling standard directly into the SysML v2 metamodel. It adds explicit uncertainty sources, characterisation, and propagation mechanisms while preserving v2 conformance. Seven case studies validate the approach.

PSUM is the first standardised uncertainty modelling specification in the MBSE community. The fact that SysML v2 doesn't natively align with it is a modelling gap, not a philosophical one. If you're modelling a system where uncertainty in a sensor input propagates through functional behaviour to a physical output, you're currently working around the language to express that.

This extension gives you a principled way to stop working around it.

Worth reading if you're doing v2 modelling on any system where uncertainty in inputs affects system behaviour.

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

#SysML #MBSE #SystemsEngineering

---

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle

SysML v2 was designed with formal semantics and composability at its core. The PSUM-SysMLv2 preprint suggests the language still has meaningful gaps for the systems that matter most right now.

Autonomous vehicles, spacecraft GNC, cyber-physical systems: these are the domains driving the current wave of MBSE adoption in aerospace and defence. They're also domains where uncertainty in system behaviour isn't a footnote. It's load-bearing.

PSUM, the OMG's standardised uncertainty specification, gives the community a shared vocabulary for uncertainty sources, characterisation, and propagation. The fact that someone needed to write a conformant SysML v2 extension to accommodate it tells you something about where the language currently sits relative to the problems it's being asked to model.

SysML v2 is not finished in the sense that matters: tooling, methodology, and language extensions are all still catching up to the domains adopting it. PSUM-SysMLv2 is one example of that work happening in the open.

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

#SysML #SystemsEngineering #MBSE

---

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

Every few months a paper proposes extending SysML. Most of them are solving real problems. Almost none of them make it into mainstream tooling.

The PSUM-SysMLv2 preprint is technically solid. Integrating the OMG uncertainty modelling standard into SysML v2 through conformant stereotypes and KerML extensions is exactly the kind of specification-level work the community needs. The authors aren't wrong about the gap.

But I've watched enough proposed extensions stall at the tooling layer to be cautious about what "valid research" translates to in practice.

SysML v2 tool support is still immature. Organisations are still figuring out baseline v2 adoption. Adding a PSUM extension layer on top of that is, for most teams right now, aspirational rather than actionable.

That's not a reason to ignore this work. It's a reason to track it, contribute to the discussion, and be honest with your programme about the adoption timeline. The problems this solves are real. The path from paper to deployed model extension is long.

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

## C3 — RAND: DoD Digital Modernisation Assessment (RRA3531-1)

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

RAND reviewed DoD's digital acquisition modernisation on behalf of Congress and found no single owner of digital acquisition policy, inconsistent leadership engagement, and weak follow-up on directives. If that matches your experience working inside a DoD programme, it should.

This isn't a think-piece. It's a congressionally-mandated technical report with findings drawn from document review and stakeholder discussions across the department.

The practical implication for systems engineers and MBSE leads is direct: when your digital engineering initiative stalls, the cause is often structural, not technical. The model isn't the problem. The absence of a sustained policy owner and resource commitment is.

RAND recommends formally chartering a steering group for digital modernisation, designating a multi-year resource commitment, and building a maturity model with outcome indicators alongside capability indicators.

Those are arguable recommendations. But the diagnosis behind them is hard to argue with.

Source: https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA3531-1.html

#DigitalEngineering #MBSE #SystemsEngineering

---

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle

DoD has published digital engineering strategies, mandates, and guidance documents for years. RAND's assessment for Congress found the execution hasn't matched the output.

No overarching strategy that's current. No single owner of digital acquisition policy. Leadership support that hasn't been consistent. Directives that weren't enforced.

For anyone working in defence systems engineering, this is familiar. The gap between what DoD digital engineering policy says and what programmes actually experience has been visible for a long time. This report puts an authoritative number on it and puts it in front of Congress.

What changes from here depends on whether the department treats the RAND recommendations as action items or as findings to be noted and filed.

The recommendation to establish a maturity model with both capability and outcome indicators is the one worth watching. Measuring digital capability without measuring outcomes is how you get impressive-looking dashboards and stalled programmes.

Source: https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA3531-1.html

#DigitalEngineering #SystemsEngineering #DoD

---

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

RAND told Congress that DoD digital acquisition has no single owner, inconsistent leadership, and weak enforcement. The defence SE community largely already knew this. The question is why it keeps being true.

Reports like this are useful. The diagnosis is real and the recommendations are reasonable. But this is not the first assessment to find that DoD digital modernisation is under-governed and under-resourced. It will probably not be the last.

The structural problem isn't a lack of findings. It's that digital engineering in DoD sits across too many stakeholders with competing equities and no one with the authority and mandate to hold the line across a full programme lifecycle.

RAND recommends chartering a steering group and developing a maturity model. Those are the right building blocks. But steering groups without sustained leadership attention become coordination bodies that produce documents.

The defence engineers and MBSE leads reading this have probably served on at least one of those. What would actually change the pattern?

Source: https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA3531-1.html

## Track 2: World Events — Defence / Energy / Geopolitics (SE Lens)

### World Event Option 1 — Requirements Volatility / Verification Gap Frame

A programme can be technically successful and still lose its requirement.

That's the ITEP story. GE's T901 engine delivers 50% more horsepower and 25% better fuel efficiency than the engine it replaces. The technology works. But the Army is now asking whether the Black Hawk and Apache need a better engine at all, given how their role is shifting in a drone-dominated battlespace. So the FY27 budget zeroed it out. Again. Congress restored funding in FY26. The Pentagon zeroed it again in FY27.

This is requirements volatility in its most expensive form. The operational context changed faster than the programme could reach Milestone C. Seven years of development, $1.5B invested, and the need statement is now contested.

The SE question most programmes don't have a clean answer to: who owns the feedback loop between evolving operational concept and active programme baseline?

Source: https://breakingdefense.com/2026/04/ge-warns-itep-engine-program-needs-little-bit-more-money-for-testing-in-fy27-budget/

### World Event Option 2 — Systems Thinking / Emergence and Feedback Loops Frame

A programme doesn't have to fail to become unnecessary.

The Army's ITEP engine programme has been zeroed in the FY27 budget request, despite the T901 engine performing exactly as designed. The problem is upstream. Army leadership is publicly questioning whether the Apache's role has fundamentally changed as drone and launched-effects platforms take on more of the traditional attack mission. If the platform's operational role shrinks, the case for a more capable engine weakens with it.

This is emergence working against a programme. The battlespace system evolved. The rotary-wing sub-system's function within that system shifted. But the development programme had no mechanism to detect that shift and feed it back into the requirement. The programme kept optimising for a need that the wider system was quietly retiring.

Multi-stakeholder conflict has kept it alive so far: Congress restores what the Pentagon cuts, allies want the engine, GE needs continuity. That's not a requirements process. That's inertia.
