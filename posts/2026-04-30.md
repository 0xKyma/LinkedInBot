An agent navigating that will produce outputs faster. It will not produce better outputs.

Before asking what an AI co-pilot can do with your model, it is worth asking whether your model gives it anything coherent to work with.

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

## Track 2: World Events — Defence / Energy / Geopolitics (SE Lens)

### World Event Option 1 — Verification Gap / Interface Management Frame

Every protection relay in the Iberian grid tripped exactly as designed. Every solar inverter responded exactly as programmed. 47 million people lost power anyway.

The ENTSO-E final report on the 28 April 2025 blackout is worth reading carefully. The root cause was not component failure. It was voltage control behaviour at the system level that nobody had verified, because the system's architecture had changed incrementally over years of renewable integration while the operational concept for grid stability stayed static.

Each generation node met its interface specification. The aggregate behaviour of those nodes together was never tested against a system-level operational envelope.

This is the verification gap that MBSE exists to close. Component conformance is not system verification. If your V&V strategy only traces requirements to components, you will miss the behaviours that emerge from their interactions.

The ENTSO-E report also notes that missing and unsynchronised fault data made root-cause sequencing impossible. No observable state means no diagnosability. That one should sting for anyone running a complex programme without a coherent data strategy.

If your programme's verification plan were reviewed today, could it demonstrate coverage of emergent system-level behaviours?

Source: https://www.entsoe.eu/publications/blackout/28-april-2025-iberian-blackout/

#SystemsEngineering #MBSE #SystemsThinking

---

### World Event Option 2 — Emergence / Unintended Consequences Frame

A grid can fail even when every part of it is working correctly. That is not a paradox. That is emergence.

One year ago today, Spain and Portugal went dark. The ENTSO-E final report confirms what systems thinkers would have predicted: the blackout resulted from interacting factors across voltage control, reactive power, protection responses, and generation disconnections. No single cause. No single guilty component.

The deeper problem is structural. Europe's energy transition added gigawatts of renewable generation to a grid whose control architecture was designed around a different kind of system. The local rules each node followed were sound. The system-level behaviour those rules produced collectively was not.

This is how complex systems fail. Not through dramatic single-point collapses, but through the slow architectural drift between a system's physical reality and the assumptions baked into its control logic. The architecture changed. The operational concept did not keep pace.

One year on, no accountability proceedings have reached a final ruling. That outcome also tells you something about how we govern systems whose failure mode is distributed across every node and owned by none.

The question worth sitting with: in the systems you work on, who owns the emergent behaviour?

Source: https://brusselssignal.eu/2026/04/spain-marks-one-year-since-europes-worst-blackout-in-decades-as-accountability-remains-elusive/
