### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

Measuring MBSE ROI will not close the evidence gap. The community keeps calling for better metrics, but that call misses why the gap exists.

The problem is structural. MBSE benefits are largely counterfactual: errors engineers caught earlier, interfaces that held, rework that never happened. You cannot measure what didn't occur without a controlled comparison programme no one will ever fund. No metrics framework fixes that.

So the community needs to stop promising empirical ROI proof it cannot produce and make a different argument: one grounded in engineering judgment, risk reduction, and design coherence. That's a harder sell, but it's an honest one.

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

SysML v2 has three active RTFs running simultaneously and zero approved resolutions between them. That is the current state of the standard as of April 2026.

RTFs for SysML 2.1, KerML 1.1, and API & Services 1.1 are all established. ## Candidate G — "Generating Logical Architectures from SysML Behaviour Models" (INCOSE SE Journal, Editor's Choice 2026)

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

Auto-generating logical architectures from SysML behaviour models would eliminate one of the most labour-intensive manual steps in MBSE. A 2026 INCOSE journal paper selected as Editor's Choice makes the case that this is achievable.

Granrath et al. describe a method for deriving logical architecture directly from SysML behaviour models. The detail of the technique is behind a Wiley paywall, but the direction is clear: if your behaviour model is rigorous enough, the structural architecture should derive from it rather than run in parallel while teams manually reconcile the two after the fact.

That "created in parallel and reconciled later" workflow is how most MBSE programmes actually run. Behaviour and structure stay loosely coupled, drift apart during design evolution, and require manual effort to re-sync. A method that collapses that gap deserves serious attention.

Request institutional access or contact the authors for a preprint.

Source: https://incose.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15206858

#MBSE #SysML #SystemsEngineering

---

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle

The boundary between behaviour modelling and architecture definition is starting to dissolve. That has real implications for how MBSE workflows get structured.

An Editor's Choice paper in the 2026 INCOSE Systems Engineering journal (Granrath et al.) proposes generating logical architectures automatically from SysML behaviour models. This sits at the intersection of two trends: increasing pressure to execute and verify models earlier, and growing toolchain capability to automate model-to-model transformations.

If behaviour models can drive architecture synthesis rather than inform it after the fact, the sequencing of MBSE activities changes. Functional analysis becomes an upstream generator, not a parallel track. That restructures how teams organise, how programmes gate reviews, and how model ownership works across disciplines.

The tooling is not there broadly yet. But the research direction is consistent enough now that programme architects should be thinking about it.

Source: https://incose.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15206858

#MBSE #SystemsEngineering #DigitalEngineering

---

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

Generating logical architectures from behaviour models sounds like automation. In practice, it is likely to automate the wrong decisions faster.

Granrath et al. propose deriving logical architecture directly from SysML behaviour models, and the paper earned Editor's Choice in the 2026 INCOSE Systems Engineering journal. The method is technically interesting. But the premise assumes that your behaviour model accurately and completely captures design intent before you commit to a logical structure.

On programmes I have worked on, that assumption fails routinely. Teams build behaviour models incrementally, with gaps, simplifications, and compromises stakeholders drove throughout the process. Generating architecture from them does not eliminate the judgment calls. It hides them inside the transformation.

The community went through this with automated code generation from UML. The generated artefacts looked right and carried wrong assumptions forward at scale.

Model-to-model automation is only as good as the model it starts from. We should be spending at least as much effort on that problem as on the automation capability itself.

Source: https://incose.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15206858

### World Event Option 2 — Emergence and Unintended Consequences Frame

Optimising for political commitment without modelling system-level consequences produces exactly the feedback loop AUKUS is now running.

The agreement created a credibility requirement: Australia needed visible allied submarine presence. So the Astute fleet began rotating through Australian ports. That rotation consumed operational availability. When the Iran conflict escalated, there was no slack in the system. HMS Anson returned early. The deployments meant to demonstrate AUKUS credibility degraded the fleet capacity that AUKUS depends on.

This is emergence. The behaviour was absent from the design. It arose from the interaction between a political commitment layer and an industrial and operational layer that nobody modelled together.

The UK Parliament inquiry frames this as a delivery problem. From a systems thinking perspective, the programme's components couple across three nations, multiple electoral cycles, and live operational theatres. Behaviour at that level of coupling rarely matches the intent of any single decision made inside it.

At what point in the AUKUS programme lifecycle would formal system architecting have caught this?
