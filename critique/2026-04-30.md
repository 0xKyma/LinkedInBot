### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

An AI coding agent just ran an RFLP workflow in System Composer. And it worked.

But I also think dismissing this as a demo would be a mistake. The tooling is real. The workflow is reproducible. That is worth paying attention to.

## Item 2: "LLM-Assisted Semantic Alignment and Integration in Collaborative MBSE Using SysML v2" — arXiv Preprint

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

Cross-organisational MBSE has always had a semantic alignment problem. LLMs might help. Or they might make it worse.

A recent arXiv preprint proposes a structured, prompt-driven approach for using GPT-based LLMs to align SysML v2 models across independently developed organisational boundaries. The idea is to use v2's alias, import, and metadata constructs as the structural foundation, then let an LLM do the interpretive heavy lifting where naming conventions, abstractions, and domain vocabularies diverge.

The paper flags the risk clearly: LLM outputs can hallucinate, and current implementations lack traceability, validation, and version control mechanisms.

That last part matters enormously for anyone on a defence or space programme. Semantic alignment that engineers cannot trace or validate is not alignment. It is a liability.

Worth reading if you're managing multi-partner SysML v2 programmes.

Source: arxiv.org/abs/2508.16181

#SysML #MBSE #SystemsEngineering

---

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle

Cross-org model integration is one of the most persistent problems in collaborative MBSE, and research is now testing whether LLMs can close the gap that v2's formal semantics opened up.

Two organisations build models independently, use different naming conventions and abstractions, then have to integrate them at programme level. This arXiv preprint argues that v2's structural modularity gives LLMs enough formal grounding to assist with semantic alignment across those boundaries.

The direction this points is significant. If v2's formal semantics can anchor LLM-assisted alignment, it strengthens the case for v2 adoption in multi-contractor programmes beyond just language modernisation. Interoperability becomes a concrete deliverable, not a theoretical benefit.

The traceability and validation gaps are real and the paper acknowledges them. But the research direction is credible and the problem it targets is one every large programme integration team knows well.

Source: arxiv.org/abs/2508.16181

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

Using an LLM to align safety-critical models across organisations sounds useful until you think about what alignment actually requires.

A new arXiv preprint proposes LLM-assisted semantic alignment for SysML v2 models in collaborative MBSE. The core idea is sound: cross-org model integration is painful, v2's formal semantics provide better grounding than v1 ever did, and LLMs can bridge gaps in naming and abstraction.

But the paper itself flags the problem. LLM outputs can hallucinate. Current implementations have no traceability, validation, or version control mechanisms.

In a commercial product development context, that might be a manageable risk. In a defence acquisition context, a semantic alignment decision that no engineer can verify or trace to a human judgement is a programme risk, full stop.

The research is worth following. But handing cross-org interface alignment to an LLM on a safety-critical programme, without a verification layer that doesn't yet exist, is not a step I'd recommend today.

## Item 3: "Uncertainty Modeling for SysML v2" — arXiv Preprint

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

SysML v2 shipped without native uncertainty constructs. This paper proposes a fix.

The OMG's Precise Semantics for Uncertainty Modelling (PSUM) is the first standardised uncertainty spec for MBSE. The problem is that SysML v2, as released, has no native constructs aligned with PSUM. So uncertainty still lives in comments, assumptions documents, and informal model annotations. For a language designed for rigour, that is a significant gap.

This arXiv preprint proposes a systematic SysML v2 extension incorporating PSUM: explicit indeterminacy sources, structured uncertainty characterisation, and consistent propagation within the model, while preserving v2 conformance. The authors validate the approach across seven case studies spanning cyber-physical and safety-critical domains.

If you're building v2 models for autonomous or safety-critical systems right now, this extension is worth reviewing. Uncertainty isn't a footnote. It's a first-class design input.

Source: arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

#SysML #MBSE #SystemsEngineering

---

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle

Every major v2 adoption conversation I've been in hits the same wall: the spec is rigorous, but it still leaves real engineering concerns to informal workarounds.

Uncertainty is a clear example. SysML v2 brought formal semantics and improved expressiveness. But it shipped without native constructs for uncertainty representation, despite the OMG having a published standard for exactly that, PSUM, already available.

A February arXiv preprint addresses this directly, proposing a conformant v2 extension that integrates PSUM into the language. Seven case studies. Explicit traceability of indeterminacy sources. Structured propagation through the model.

This signals something broader: the community isn't waiting for the next spec revision to close gaps. Practitioners and researchers are building extensions now. The question for programmes evaluating v2 adoption is whether they track and adopt those extensions, or discover the gaps after the modelling investment is already made.

Source: arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

#SysML #MBSE #DigitalEngineering

---

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle

We built a more formally rigorous modelling language and still shipped it without a way to represent uncertainty.

SysML v2 is a genuine improvement. Better formal semantics, cleaner syntax, proper API support. The community worked hard on it and it shows.

But the OMG's own uncertainty modelling standard, PSUM, exists and the OMG published it. And SysML v2 launched without native constructs aligned to it. So uncertainty, one of the most consequential inputs to any system design decision, still has no first-class home in the language.

A new arXiv preprint proposes a v2 extension that fixes this. It's good work and the seven case studies are convincing.

We should also ask why uncertainty never made it into scope from the start. Standardisation is slow and consensus is hard, and that is a fair answer. But it is still an answer worth sitting with.

Source: arxiv.org/abs/2602.21641

### World Event Option 1 — Verification Gap / Requirements Baselining Frame

The US munitions stockpile didn't fail. It performed exactly as designed. Nobody ever validated that design against this kind of operation.

Pentagon comptroller Jules Hurst confirmed to Congress that Operation Epic Fury cost $25 billion, mostly munitions. More than 850 Tomahawks fired. Half the Patriot interceptor stocks. Half the THAAD inventory. 80% of JASSM-ER. Replenishment: two to five years.

This is a verification gap at national scale.

Procurement optimised around unit cost, not production-rate capacity. The requirements addressed deterrence scenarios and limited strike concepts. Nobody formally validated those requirements against a sustained high-intensity air campaign. When the operational context shifted, the gap became $25 billion and a multi-year strategic vulnerability.

We do this on programmes too. We verify against the contract spec and call it done. We rarely ask whether the operational scenario the spec assumed is still the right one.

If you last reviewed your requirements baseline three years ago, when did you last validate it against what the system might actually face?

### World Event Option 2 — Unintended Consequences / System Optimisation for the Wrong Objective

A system that optimises for one objective will underperform against a different one. That sounds obvious until you are the one who approved the requirements.

The $25 billion bill for Operation Epic Fury is not just a budget story. It shows what happens when a procurement system chases cost efficiency over throughput capacity, across years of policy decisions that each made sense in isolation.

The US fired more Tomahawks in 71 hours than procurement delivered in roughly two and a half years. Each decision to prioritise unit-cost reduction over production-rate investment looked rational locally. Together, those decisions produced a stockpile that depleted faster than industry can replenish it.

That is the pattern systems thinking exists to catch. Feedback loops between operational consumption rates and industrial base capacity. Reinforcing dynamics between low procurement targets and shrinking supplier bases. Engineers could observe those interactions. The programme just never treated them as a system.

Replenishment now takes two to five years. That is the cost of optimising components without owning the system behaviour.
