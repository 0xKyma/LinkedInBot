## MBSE Fundamentals Training

### Option 1 — Practitioner angle

SysML syntax took most cohort members about a day to pick up. That part wasn't the challenge.

The hard part was getting them to think through decomposition and traceability before they opened the tool. How you frame the problem determines how coherent the model turns out. No amount of notation fluency compensates for weak thinking upstream.

Eight days into delivering this fundamentals training, that pattern has shown up consistently across every cohort block.

Short courses that jump straight to tool navigation skip the step that actually matters. Engineers leave knowing how to draw diagrams. They don't leave knowing why they drew them that way, or whether the structure they chose serves the design problem.

That's the gap worth closing.

### Option 2 — Industry/trend angle (The adoption gap)

Tool access doesn't close the MBSE skills gap. Engineers can get a licence and follow a tutorial in an afternoon.

What's harder to close is the gap between tool proficiency and design thinking. Eight days into delivering MBSE fundamentals training, and the same finding repeats across cohorts: syntax is easy, structure is hard. Engineers need to know how to decompose a system and build traceable models before the tool becomes useful. That takes more than a two-day software walkthrough.

Most MBSE training on the market targets the wrong problem. Vendors have an incentive to teach tool use. Nobody has a strong commercial incentive to teach systems thinking.

That's worth paying attention to when you're scoping training for your programme.

#MBSE #SystemsEngineering #SysML

---

### Option 3 — Contrarian angle (The mainstream fix makes it worse)

Better tooling will not fix the MBSE skills gap. More of it might widen it.

Eight days into a fundamentals training block, and every cohort confirms the same thing. Engineers learn SysML notation fast. What takes sustained effort is learning to structure a design problem before they touch the tool. That's a thinking skill, and you can't abstract it away with a better interface.

The push toward low-code modelling environments and auto-generation features is aimed at reducing the barrier to entry. But if engineers never develop the underlying mental model for decomposition and traceability, they'll produce faster and more confident models that are just as incoherent.

Ease of use is not the same as quality of thought.
